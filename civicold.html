The draft of FixGov.

This draft will focus on our our capacity to build alternative lifestyles at grassroots level. In this chapter we will press forward the need for changing of our way of life: meeting the challenges of a new frontier.

Today the people of the world are challenged with unprecedented problems as improper care and protection for the earth's ecological systems have brought us to the brink of collapse threatening our life support system.

At the same time the population soars bringing increasing demands to bear upon these fragile and interconnected systems. In addition, technological advances are to the point where human labor forces are increasingly irrelevant to the production of goods. Labor forces have thus become delinked from the financial markets for the first time in history as civilization proceeds from the industrial age into the age of knowledge -- an advent which may leave millions of people behind with no means of sustenance.

How to address these issues has become the question of the day, particularly as those who are seemingly 'in charge' proceed down the path of globalization -- a path which disregards the needs of people and the environment as it chases the wheel of wealth and fortunes for the few while ignoring the needs of the many.

This has resulted in over 51% of the world's wealth being held in the hands of approximately 200 people today while millions of others worldwide suffer from unbearable poverty with consequent hardships bordering on deliberate inhumane treatment.

There is sufficient new evidence that the predicament we face today was brought about through 'social engineering' practices first initiated by secret societies at about the same time that gold was discovered and 'wealth creation' came into fashion. These 'social engineering' practices, which some refer to as the 'dominator paradigm' were soon being practiced under the guidance of religious and other educational orders by royal command and continue even today through our churches and institutions of learning.

Disseminated broadly these 'practices' have formed the basis of relationship between the earth and humans over thousands of years. They still serve today as ecomomic needs supercede the natural order of earth needs as taught in our schools and practiced and applied through politics and government as 'systems thinking' where the well-being of the earth and the humans who inhabit it are subservient to the needs of a few for wealth creation.

Under the guidance of the wealthy few, the modern world, centered in the U.S., has devised the 'perfect' system of capitalism under which to dominate both the earth and the majority of the people on it. Capitalism thrives on competition and seeks to devour everything in its path as it 'grows' new market economies which ravage the earth's resources and dehumanize people. Yet so complete has been the immersion of people into this system that few recognize it for the cannibal that it is.

This is of great danger -- the so-called 'Green Revolution' guaranteeing increased food production; which promoted the sale and use of chemical fertilizers, pesticides and herbicides, monocropping, cattle-raising for beef production and deforestation for the benefit of a few, is largely responsible for increasing desertification as a result of worldwide topsoil losses. Since civilization itself is dependent upon the topsoil on which it rests, one can readily see how we are digging the foundation out from under our home. Now, the same 'experts' who promoted the green revolution threaten us through 'bioengineering' practices.

However, with the new found recognition that the earth's systems are collapsing with global warming and desertification becoming real threats to all human life, a growing number of those in the civil service sector are questioning capitalism, globalization and continued technological advances as being the 'effective' and 'efficient' systems upon which we can rely for security as touted by the 'technocrats.'

As happens in systems when they go 'awray' sometimes, there appears to be a spontaneous awakening among some members of the civil society, mainly women, who are identified as 'inner directed' and entitled either 'cultural creatives' or 'integral culturists' that there is something dramatically wrong that must be addressed. This has resulted in millions of individuals around the world looking askance at the stability and security of our present systems and beginning to 'link' with like-minded others to explore the situation.

As a result there is an increasing movement toward the creation of 'sustainable living' societies based in decentralized financial systems, governance through bioregionalism, and lessening of world trade among the civil society sector both in the developed and the developing worlds. There is an awareness of those involved that competition is antithematic to sustainable living and that cooperation is a 'given.'

However, a turn in the direction of sustainable living requires that society examine its old thought patterns and institute lifestyle transformations based on systems that more nearly fit the needs of today for well being among all its members. This, in essence, is a new frontier -- whether or not we can meet the challenge of crossing into this new frontier with all of its implications in a timely manner will largely determine the future of the human race.

The hoped for result is the construction of sustainable living communities being built within each bioregion that would ultimately replace our non-sustainable cities of today. Each community would be self-governing and form a 'regional communities' group which would in turn form associated regional communities groups. Governance would emanate from these regional communities groups and establish an international governance group of some form which could be based on a restructured United Nations (renamed) or some new organization. This could give us a highly 'decentralized' governance with control at the community level instead of from either the state, national, or international level. It just seems to me that 'sustainable living' communities are also 'self-managing' communities with community members willing and able to take responsibility for their actions. So little governance is necessary in this type of arrangement.
